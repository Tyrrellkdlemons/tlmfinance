Banking 101

Checking vs savings vs prepaid.

Checking — for paying bills and getting paid. Comes with a debit card.

Savings — money you don't spend. Pays a small interest rate.

Prepaid card — you load money on it, then spend. No bank account required, but watch the fees.

If you've been denied a regular checking account, ask any branch about a "second-chance" or "fresh-start" account (see below).

Credit

Your credit score is a story, not a sentence.

It tracks: do you pay on time? How much of your available credit do you use? How long have your accounts been open?

Pull your free credit report (all 3 bureaus) at AnnualCreditReport.com. Look for accounts you didn't open — identity theft is common after incarceration.

A secured credit card (you put down a deposit) is the most common path to building credit from zero or rebuilding it.

Savings

Start with a $250 starter fund.

Don't aim for "three months of expenses" yet. Aim for $250 you don't touch. Then $500. Then a month of rent.

The point isn't the size — it's the gap between you and a payday loan.

Most banks will let you set up a small auto-transfer (e.g. $10/week from checking → savings). Make it automatic and stop thinking about it.

Money apps

Cash App, Venmo, Zelle, PayPal.

These move money between people fast. They are not savings accounts and not always FDIC-insured.

Rule: keep day-to-day balances small inside these apps. Move payroll into a bank or credit union; move only what you need out to spend or send.

If a "buyer" on a marketplace insists you use Cash App or Zelle, that's almost always a scam. Real buyers will pay through the marketplace's protected flow.

Crypto

Crypto in 60 seconds.

Bitcoin / Ethereum — like digital cash whose ownership is recorded on a public ledger. Prices swing a lot, both up and down.

Stablecoins (USDC, USDT) — try to track the dollar 1:1. Less volatile, but not FDIC-insured.

NFTs — digital ownership receipts; mostly speculative.

Honest rule: don't put rent money in crypto. Treat it like a tiny side bet, not a savings plan. Anyone "guaranteeing" returns is lying.

Coinbase, Kraken, and Cash App all let you buy a few dollars of Bitcoin if you want to learn by doing. Start with $5.

AI literacy

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — your free thinking partner.

What they're great at: writing a resume, drafting an email, explaining a bill or contract, practicing a job interview, summarizing a long document, learning to code.

What they're bad at: live facts (use search instead), exact math (verify every number), legal/medical decisions (use them for first-pass info, not for the final call).

Privacy: never paste full SSN, government ID numbers, or full bank account numbers into a chatbot.

Try this prompt: "I'm a returning citizen with a [your skill] background looking for a fair-chance employer in [your city]. Suggest 5 search strategies and 5 sample outreach messages."

Online safety

Scams to spot in your first 90 days.

  • Job scam — they want you to "buy equipment" or cash a check. Real jobs never ask you to pay.
  • Romance / friendship scam — someone you've never met asks for money or crypto. Always.
  • Phishing texts — "your package is held," "your bank locked your card." Open the app yourself, don't click.
  • Identity theft — pull your credit report; freeze it free at all 3 bureaus.
  • Fake debt collector — real ones must send written notice you can verify.

Second-chance banking

If you've been denied a regular account.

Many banks and credit unions offer "second-chance," "opportunity," or "fresh-start" accounts. They cost a few dollars a month, usually graduate to a full account after 6–12 months of clean activity, and are reported to ChexSystems differently than a closed account.

Credit unions are often the kindest place to start — they're member-owned and many partner with reentry organizations.

Taxes

Don't fear April.

Most people benefit from filing — even with low income — because of refundable credits like the EITC.

Free help: VITA sites and IRS Free File.

If you owe back taxes from before incarceration, the IRS has installment plans and hardship options — call them before they call you.

Paychecks

Read every line of your pay stub.

"Gross" is the offer. "Net" is what hits your account.

Watch for: federal income tax, state income tax, FICA (Social Security + Medicare), health insurance, garnishments. If a line you didn't agree to keeps showing up, ask HR.
